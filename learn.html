What is a tree-sequence

A succinct tree sequence, or tree sequence for short, represents the relationships between a set of DNA sequences. Tree sequences can be used to store genetic data efficiently, and enable powerful and rapid analysis of millions of whole genomes at a time. They can be created by simulation or by inferring relationships from genetic variation.

The key things a tree sequence can provide are:
A record of full genetic ancestry
Tree sequences efficiently store the full genetic history of a set of genomes, by sharing edges (branches) between adjacent trees. Read more ...
An encoding of DNA data
Placing mutations on a tree sequence allows lossless representation and compression of DNA datasets. Read more ...
An efficient analysis framework
Analyses of genetic variation can be reformulated as measurements on trees; applying this to trees with shared structure can (massively?) speed up genome-wide statistical calculations. Read more ...

A record of full genetic ancestry

Two basic principles underpin the tree sequence concept:

  1. At a given position along a chromosome, the relationship between a set of genomes can be represented by a single evolutionary tree.
  2. Trees near to each other on the chromosome look similar, that is they share structure.
A visualization of a short tree sequence is shown below, focussing on the first 1000 letters (base pairs) of 20 simulated genomes. These genomes are represented as blue dots at the tips of each tree; the position of the trees along the chromosome is indicated underneath. The relationship, or genealogy, linking the 20 genomes is the same between positions 0 and 383, and is shown by the first tree. This is slightly different from the genealogy between positions 384 and 874 (the second tree), which is again slightly different from the genealogy between positions 875 and 999 (the third tree). Nevertheless the three trees are almost identical, as highlighted by the shared, coloured branches.

A tree sequence captures the shared structure between trees by allowing edges (the branches or lines in the trees) to persist from one tree to another. This is equivalent to storing the difference between adjacent trees. Differences between adjacent trees are usually very small, even for huge trees, which explains why tree sequences are so efficient.

An encoding of DNA data

Knowing the full ancestry of all the parts of the genome can be incredibly informative, but how does this translate into the actual letters of a DNA sequence? The key is that differences between genomes are caused by mutations, and once you know the tree, usually only a few mutations are needed to explain the pattern of variation.

This is essentially an evolutionary encoding of the data.
